Subject to the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, the Schrödinger equation for a system of $N$ electrons takes the form\footnote{Using atmoic units, $4\pi\epsilon_0=\hbar=m_e=e=1$.}
\begin{align}
\hat{H} \Psi(\R) = \left[-\sum_{i=1}^N \frac{\nabla_i^2}{2m_e} + V_\text{external}(\r_i) + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \frac{1}{|\r_i-\r_j|}\right] \Psi(\R) = E\Psi(\R).
\end{align}

\subsection{Outline of the Hartree-Fock Method}
The Hartree-Fock method is one of the most important models in all of quantum chemistry, not only because it may yield acceptable approximations in certain scenarios, but because it is also an important stepping stone on the way to more accurate methods. Only a few of the more sophisticated quantum chemistry methods bypass HF entirely, while \emph{most} of them use it as a first step and then build on the HF orbitals to obtain more accurate descriptions (Szabo, p108).

The Hartree-Fock method is a \emph{mean field} method (Fys-kjm4480, p61) because it treats the inter electron interaction only in an averaged way. Any single electron does not feel the effect of every other localized electron, but rather just an averaged potential from all of remaining ones. This is sometimes also called an \emph{independent-particle} model. 

The method itself essentially finds the most energetically favorable electronic wave function, under the assumption that the full ground state consists of \emph{a single} Slater determinant populated by orthonormal spin-orbitals, $\phi_i$. We denote this Slater determinant $|\Psi_0\rangle$,
\begin{align}
\psiz &= |\phi_0\phi_1\phi_2\dots\phi_{N-1}\phi_{N}\rangle, \ \ \ \langle \phi_i|\phi_j\rangle = \delta_{ij},
\end{align}
